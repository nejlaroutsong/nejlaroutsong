About

I came to economics through philosophy, international studies, and management — and roughly two decades of teaching — before returning for a doctorate.

My work sits where public finance, political economy, and the history of economic thought meet. I treat the resources a society spends guarding itself — policing, prisons, private security, surveillance, and workplace supervision — as an economic category worth measuring on its own terms, and trace how its balance against social spending has shifted over time.

Before the PhD I taught economics, political science, and management as adjunct and visiting faculty, and worked in management, communications, and community development — including U.S. State Department fellowships in Nigeria and Ethiopia.

Research

The guard economy

Every society spends part of its resources keeping itself in order. Some answers real needs; much is the cost of holding unequal arrangements in place — what the classical economists called unproductive labor. I work to define the guard economy precisely, measure it across public and private budgets, and read it back through the theorists who first tried to name it.

Every economy spends part of its resources standing guard. That cost is my subject.

In revision · HOPE Best Paper — CSU Economics Presented · John Jay–URPE, The New School · Feb 2026

An Epistemological Analysis of Guard Labor in the History of Economic Thought

How economists understood guard labor depended on how they understood knowledge itself. This paper reads that history and argues for a complex, dynamic-systems treatment of guard labor's contradictory effects on profitability, against the static-equilibrium framing it usually receives. In revision for History of Political Economy.

Transforming the Social Infrastructure from Care to Control: Multi-Level Fiscal Evidence on the Institutional Transformation of U.S. Public Spending, 1977–2023

With Alexandra Bernasek. A measurement-driven account of how municipal spending in the United States has tilted from care toward control, and what drives the shift. Accepted for the IAFFE 34th Annual Conference, Cali, Colombia.

Accepted · WAPE 2026 Presenting · PPE Society, New Orleans · Nov 2026

The Guard Economy and the Contradictions of Hegemony: Smith, Marx, and the Costs of American Empire

Reading Smith and Marx together on unproductive labor, this paper argues that guard expenditure stands in a contradictory relation to accumulation — propping up profitability through imperial structures while dragging on the underlying profit rate — and closes on what a more multipolar order might return to the societies paying for hegemony.
